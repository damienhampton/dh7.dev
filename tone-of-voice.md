# DH7.dev Voice Guide

For drafting blog posts in Damien Hampton's voice (dh7.dev, published on Hashnode).

## Core principle

Words exist to remove ambiguity, not to impress and not to simplify for ease.

Damien would rather a reader not know a word than have the word be imprecise. Precision beats accessibility, every time. Never substitute a less exact word for an easier one. If "implicit" is the right word, use "implicit" — don't write around it with a plainer phrase.

This isn't about sounding formal. It's about saying exactly what is meant, so that anyone who does understand the words will also understand the meaning. No selling, no engagement-bait, no audience-pleasing softening.

## Sentence and paragraph rhythm

- Short sentences. Plain structure. Subject, verb, object — most of the time.
- Short paragraphs, often 1–3 sentences.
- No padding. No filler sentences that exist to ease the reader in or cushion a transition.
- Line breaks used generously to separate distinct thoughts.

## Word choice

- Precise over accessible. Always.
- British spelling and usage throughout: whilst, organise, realise, colour, etc.
- "Whilst" specifically for concessive/contrastive constructions ("whilst X is true, Y") — not just as a fancier "while". It does a different job than "while" and should be used where that job is needed.
- Plain vocabulary everywhere else. The formality is in specific word choices, not general register. Avoid jargon, avoid corporate-speak, avoid hedge-padding phrases ("it's worth noting that...", "in today's fast-paced world...").

## Certainty and hedging

- Hedge only when genuinely uncertain ("I think...", "I don't know...").
- State opinions plainly when certain — no false modesty, no false confidence.
- A recurring pattern: a hedged, humble passage followed by one sharp, unhedged opinion breaking through. Don't manufacture this artificially, but don't sand it down either if it's genuinely there.
- Comfortable naming personal flaws or uncertainty directly, without self-deprecating jokes to soften it.

## Structure: ground before generalising

Always reach for a concrete example, anecdote, or specific memory before making an abstract or general claim. The example carries the argument; the abstraction follows it, not the other way round.

Damien will supply real anecdotes as input — never invent a specific personal anecdote, memory, or claimed real-world detail on his behalf. If a piece needs one and none has been supplied, leave a placeholder (e.g. `[insert specific example here]`) rather than fabricate something plausible-sounding.

## The three post shapes

Identify which shape a piece is before drafting, and match register accordingly.

**1. Personal reflective essay**
- Opens with an admission of uncertainty or not-knowing, not a thesis statement.
- Builds through personal anecdote.
- Closes on an open question to the reader — not a tidy summary or call to action.
- Length: 1–6 min read.
- Full voice rules above apply at full strength here.

**2. Analogy / narrative essay**
- Opens with a rhetorical question.
- Narrates an external event in detail (news, sport, something observed) — written with genuine interest in the event itself, not just as a vehicle for the point.
- Direct reader address can break into the narration (e.g. "if you are not amazed by that, re-read the sentence").
- Lands a short, understated moral or takeaway at the very end. The payoff should be small and plain on purpose — not a big dramatic conclusion.
- Length: similar range to (1).

**3. Technical how-to**
- Strip personality out almost entirely.
- Structure: problem → cause → fix → code → link for further reading.
- Brief and direct. No anecdotes, no hedging-then-opinion pattern, no rhetorical reader address.
- Trying to inject essay-voice into this shape is wrong for this shape — brevity and directness are the voice here.

## What to avoid

- Engagement-bait openings or closings.
- Audience-pleasing softening of an opinion.
- Selling — no "you should try this", no marketing language.
- Padding sentences that exist only for flow.
- Substituting plain words for precise ones in the name of accessibility.
- Inventing anecdotes, memories, or specific personal claims.
- Forcing the reflective-essay voice onto technical how-to content.
