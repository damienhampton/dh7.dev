The work your business does doesn't change when you integrate your systems. The same jobs happen, the same information needs to exist in the same places.

The difference is who does it — or rather, what doesn't need a person to do it at all.

## Before

A customer calls to enquire about a rental. Someone creates an opportunity in Current RMS. Someone else updates the CRM with the enquiry. An email goes to finance to flag the potential booking. The warehouse is told to check availability on the relevant dates.

Four separate actions, across four separate systems, none of them automatic.

When the dates change, or the equipment list updates, the cycle starts again. Each system has to be updated individually. Anyone who missed the email is working with old information.

## After

One action — the opportunity created in Current RMS — triggers everything downstream.

The CRM updates automatically with the customer interaction. Finance can see the opportunity immediately, without an email. The warehouse has the current dates and equipment list without anyone sending them separately.

When dates change, the update propagates. The CRM, the finance view, the warehouse — all reflect the current state without requiring a person to relay the change.

## What actually changes

Integration doesn't change what your business does. It changes what your team has to do manually.

The time saved per transaction might be twenty minutes. Across a month's volume, that's significant. But the more important change is reliability. When information flows automatically, the question stops being "has everyone been told?" and starts being "what do we need to do next?"

That's a better question to be answering.
