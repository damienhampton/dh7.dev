Most businesses didn't design their software stack. They assembled it over time. A booking or rental system that handled the core workflow. A CRM to manage customer relationships. An accounting platform. A scheduling or routing tool. Something for internal communication.

Each was chosen because it was the best available option for its specific job. That's a reasonable way to build a stack.

The problem is that none of them know what the others are doing.

## The gap between systems is where problems live

A customer updates their delivery address. Your sales team records it in the CRM. The booking system still has the old one. The driver shows up at the wrong place.

Nobody made a mistake. The right information exists in one system. It just didn't make it to another.

This happens constantly in businesses that run on disconnected tools. Not because the tools are bad — they're often very good at what they do — but because the tools don't share a view of the world.

## Having the information is not the same as having consistent, accessible information

Your team have access to five platforms. The data across them describes the same customers, the same jobs, the same transactions. But each system has its own version of that data, updated at different times, in different formats, by different people.

The result: you can't be sure which version to trust. Checking takes time. Mistakes slip through.

## Integration doesn't mean replacing your systems

The instinct when tools don't work together is often to look for a single platform that does everything. That trade-off rarely works well. A system that covers the full stack tends to do each piece less well than a specialist tool.

Integration keeps the specialist tools and connects them. When something changes in one system, the relevant parts of the others update automatically. The CRM and the booking system agree on the customer's address. The accounting platform sees the same jobs that operations does.

The tools still do separate jobs. They just do them with the same information.
