Follow a single customer record through a typical service business.

A customer fills in a form on your website. Name, company, address, contact details. That information goes into your CRM — either automatically, or someone types it in from a notification email.

When they book a job, someone creates a record in the operations or rental system. The customer's details go in again. Same name, same address, same contact number.

When the job is invoiced, the accounting platform needs a contact record. Entered again, this time in the format the accounting software expects.

The driver gets a job sheet. The customer address is on it — printed from the operations system, or typed into a separate document.

That is four or five separate entries of the same information. Most businesses haven't counted this because each individual entry takes a minute or two. It doesn't feel significant.

## Multiply it out

Take a conservative estimate. Two minutes per entry, four entries per customer, 20 new customers per month. That's 160 minutes of data entry per month on customer records alone — before you count order details, delivery addresses, special instructions, or any of the other fields that get re-entered at each handoff.

It adds up. But the time isn't the main cost.

## Every re-entry is another opportunity for an error

An address typed slightly differently. A company name that doesn't match between systems. A phone number with a digit transposed.

None of these errors is catastrophic in isolation. But they compound. The invoice goes to the wrong contact. The driver has an outdated address. The CRM search doesn't find the customer because the name is formatted differently in each system.

The cost isn't the entry time. It's the inconsistency introduced each time. Downstream, someone has to find the discrepancy, work out which version is correct, and fix it. Often after something has already gone wrong.

## The alternative

One entry. The customer fills in a form, or the first person to speak with them creates a record. That record propagates to every system that needs it. Updates flow the same way.

No re-entry. No discrepancies to resolve. The information is the same everywhere because it only exists once.
