A job gets marked complete in the field. In most service businesses, what happens next requires at least one person to do something.

Someone has to update the operations system to reflect completion. Someone has to create the invoice. Someone has to notify the customer that the work is done. Someone has to update the CRM so the account manager has a current picture.

Each of these is a handoff. And handoffs are where things fall through.

## What the failures look like

The customer doesn't get invoiced because admin wasn't told the job was done. [insert example — e.g. job completed on a Friday afternoon, field team didn't log it until Monday, invoice went out a week late]

The CRM still shows the job as pending a fortnight later when the account manager calls to check in.

The driver's notes — additional work carried out, access issues, customer signature — never made it to the customer file.

None of these require anyone to have made a deliberate mistake. They're the natural result of information moving through people rather than through systems.

## What it looks like when the handoffs are automated

When the job is marked complete, the invoice is created automatically with the correct line items. The customer receives notification. The CRM is updated. The driver's notes are attached to the customer record.

None of this requires a handoff. There's no person in the middle to forget, to be busy, to have gone home for the weekend.

The time between job done and invoice sent is measured in seconds rather than days.

## Where value leaks

The gap between job complete and money in is where a significant amount of value leaks in service businesses. Jobs invoiced late. Jobs not invoiced at all because they fell through the gap. Cash flow affected because billing is a manual process that depends on people having time.

Closing that gap isn't about doing more. It's about removing the dependency on a person to relay information that could move automatically.
