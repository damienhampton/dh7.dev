You can usually tell when software is no longer fit for purpose. It's slow, it crashes, it can't do what you need. You know it needs replacing.

A process is harder to diagnose. It works — in the sense that things get done. It's just that the doing of them has quietly become expensive, fragile, or both.

Here are five signs the process is the problem.

## 1. You have a person whose main job is moving information between systems

Not someone who does a bit of data entry alongside other work. Someone for whom copying information from one system to another is the job description, or close to it.

This role tends to grow incrementally. It starts as a small part of someone's day and becomes their whole day over a period of months. By the time you notice, it's hard to unpick.

## 2. Month-end reconciliation takes days

If closing out the month requires significant manual effort to reconcile what one system says against another, the problem isn't the reconciliation process. The problem is that the underlying data has been diverging all month.

[insert example — e.g. invoices in Current RMS not matching what's in Xero, requiring line-by-line comparison each month]

When systems are connected properly, reconciliation becomes a check rather than a task.

## 3. Your team asks each other for status updates that live in a system somewhere

"Do you know if that delivery has gone out?" "Has the invoice been sent?" "Is that job confirmed?"

These questions have answers — they're in the operations system, the CRM, the accounting platform. The fact that someone is asking another person means either the information isn't where it should be, or it's there but nobody trusts it.

Both are symptoms of the same thing: systems that don't stay in sync.

## 4. Mistakes happen at the handoffs, not inside the systems themselves

The booking system works fine. The accounting package works fine. The errors appear when information moves between them.

Wrong contact name on an invoice. Delivery going to an old address. Job booked under a different company name than the one in the CRM.

If your error log is dominated by handoff problems rather than data quality problems, the gap between your systems is the issue.

## 5. You've built spreadsheets to bridge gaps between tools

A spreadsheet that lives alongside your systems and fills a gap they don't cover between them. Someone maintains it. It gets emailed around. It occasionally drifts from reality.

Spreadsheets like this are a sign that your tools don't quite meet in the middle, and someone has improvised a solution. That solution has a maintenance cost and a reliability cost that aren't always obvious.

---

None of these are signs of failure. They're signs of growth. Your business has expanded past what your original processes could comfortably handle. The question is what you do next.

The answer usually isn't new software. It's connecting what you already have.
