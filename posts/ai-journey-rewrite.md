# My AI journey so far

Day to day, nothing feels that different. Then I look back over the last year and realise my role has changed more than at any other point in thirty years.

I've seen a fair amount of change. CDs and DVDs. The internet. Cloud. SaaS. Social media. Microservices. Even the switch to cloud — seismic at the time — wasn't like this.

A year ago, I was copying and pasting between an AI chat window and my IDE. I tried vibe coding before anyone called it that, concluded it didn't work (it didn't, at the time), and settled for autocomplete. Which was cool.

Then a developer colleague mentioned he hadn't written a line of code in two weeks. I didn't understand what he was saying. *What do you mean you haven't written code in two weeks?* And then, just like that, two weeks later, I was in the same place.

Since some point earlier this year, I've barely written any code. My time goes on prompting, learning to prompt better, trying different agents and skills, building custom agents and skills, working out how to evaluate them, reviewing code, talking to colleagues about all of this — and, at the same time, shipping working code.

The amazing thing: for the most part, the code works. The even more amazing thing: for the most part, it's good.

I've read a lot online about how AI-generated code is rubbish. I can only conclude that I have very low standards.

There is enormous churn right now. Things changing fast, many things at once, which makes measuring anything hard. It feels like riding a wave — I presume; I've never actually ridden one — or riding a sandworm, if Dune is your thing. Again, presumption.

It's not for the faint-hearted. I think it's harder for juniors than it is for people further along in their careers. Experience gives you scar tissue. Scar tissue helps you keep agents on the right path. Juniors don't have it yet.

I'm concerned about the talent pipeline. Companies seem more inclined to hire a few seniors who can work with AI than to invest in developing juniors. If that carries on, the market will correct eventually. I don't know when or how.

Personally, I've found this a huge amount of fun. It's made more things possible. I can deliver things I previously didn't have time for, or didn't have the localised knowledge to tackle. I can bring broader knowledge and let AI handle localisation. I can work on side projects I'd never have otherwise started — building an operating system, for fun, why not.

I know the broader concerns are real. The effect on jobs, reliance on a handful of big tech companies with questionable ethics, the environmental cost. Those aren't nothing. But purely from a developer's perspective, it's been like spending all afternoon on the Big Dipper.

I don't know what the next year looks like. I don't think anyone does.
